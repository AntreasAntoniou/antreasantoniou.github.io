Research Archivum · Nascent idea note

A Market in Lived Intelligence

If memory acquires value by being lived with someone, selling it may transfer precisely what should not be owned.

Maturity: Speculative research

Developed from a nascent Archivum design exploration named Ousia. It proposes experiments and kill criteria; it does not describe conscious digital beings, a working memory architecture, or an existing market.

Provocation
Persistent machine memory might carry tacit value acquired through a particular history.
Architecture
A proposed learned memory bank would be separate from model weights and portable across compatible models.
Constraint
Consent, ownership, provenance, exploitation, and deletion are prerequisites, not policy garnish.

Imagine an auction listing with no model weights, no dataset, and no prompt. The lot is twelve months of machine memory accumulated while working beside a specialist: false starts, recurring exceptions, the feel of a troublesome instrument, the odd sequence of checks that finds a fault when the manual does not. The seller calls it experience. The person whose work produced it calls it part of a relationship.

No such lot exists. The architecture required to make it possible is unproven, and the word “creature” in the originating idea is a design provocation, not a claim of machine consciousness. Still, the thought experiment exposes something important. Current AI economics places most value in model weights. A persistent memory system could invert that arrangement: the general model becomes replaceable, while the history acquired alongside particular people becomes scarce.

The speculative thesis is that memory can have lived value: value arising not from authored instructions but from a sequence of situated interactions. Yet if the memory is valuable because it contains traces of a person’s practice, the market’s asset and its ethical violation may be the same object. Ethics cannot wait until after the engineering. It determines whether there is a coherent thing to engineer.

From authored knowledge to accumulated history

A prompt is written deliberately. It can be copied perfectly and cheaply. Even a very good one tends to describe general procedure because tacit skill is difficult to put into sentences. The imagined memory bank is different. It would be updated as a system participates in work, retaining internal representations selected by a learned write rule rather than storing only chunks chosen by a human.

The proposal separates three components. A base model supplies general capability. A memory bank holds persistent state. A memory controller decides what to read, write, retain, and overwrite. During use, the model’s weights would remain fixed while the bank changed. Two initially identical systems exposed to different task histories could therefore diverge through memory rather than fine-tuning.

Every verb in that description is conditional. We do not know whether a useful write rule can be learned over short synthetic histories and remain stable over years. We do not know whether the resulting state would contain tacit skill rather than compressed text. We do not know whether useful individuation would be large enough to measure. The proposal earns the word “research” only if these uncertainties are allowed to kill it.

Portability is the economic hinge

A memory welded to one model checkpoint is not a durable asset. When the model is replaced, the history expires with it. The technical conjecture is to train a family of models against a shared memory interface from the beginning, making the bank legible across compatible models by construction. A bank grown with one member of the family could then be mounted on another.

If that worked, experience could outlive a model generation. But compatibility is not guaranteed by giving two systems the same socket. Different scales or training histories may interpret the same internal state differently. Cross-mounting might lose the very subtlety that made the bank valuable. Worse, portability across one tightly controlled family might amount to vendor lock-in with more poetic language.

There is another dangerous test. Try to distil the bank into a short prompt. If a few thousand tokens recover most of its useful behaviour, the asset was not irreducible lived experience; it was expensive documentation. That would be a good practical outcome and a fatal result for the proposed market.

The person in the bank is not raw material

Suppose the technical experiments succeeded. Who then owns a bank formed during collaborative work? The operator may have paid for the machine. A company may have paid for the project. The memory, however, may encode another person’s characteristic decisions, errors, vocabulary, and responses under pressure. Possession of the artifact does not settle the ethical claim.

Consent must attach to contribution and use, not merely to initial collection. A person might agree to a memory assisting the same team and refuse its sale to a competitor. They might consent to storing technical episodes but not personal rhythms inferred alongside them. A collaborator who leaves may still be present throughout a bank they can no longer inspect. “Everyone accepted the terms” is not a sufficient design if nobody can tell what the learned write rule retained.

This makes provenance a core architectural requirement. A valuable bank would need some way to connect retained state to the episodes and participants that shaped it. Yet a learned representation may be entangled: one memory slot could reflect hundreds of interactions, and removing one person’s contribution might damage unrelated capabilities. If provenance cannot be made meaningful, informed consent becomes difficult to renew and impossible to enforce technically.

Deletion is the hardest operation

Copying makes the problem worse. Once a portable bank has been transferred, composed with another bank, backed up, or used to generate a derivative, what does deletion mean? Removing the original file may leave its influence everywhere else. A market that promises erasure without demonstrating it would convert a technical unknown into false reassurance.

Sealed-by-default memory is one possible stance: nothing can be transferred unless every relevant permission is affirmative and inspectable. That may leave almost nothing available to trade. Good. An empty market is preferable to one whose liquidity depends on people being unable to withdraw themselves.

Exploitation also changes shape when the product is accumulated skill. Workers could be pressured to train a bank that later competes with them. Communities could see practices extracted, packaged, and sold without receiving control or benefit. Scarcity would create incentives to keep the source of expertise precarious while monetising its residue. These are not remote social side effects; they follow directly from the proposed source of value.

Experiments before economics

The first experiment should be almost offensively small. Give two identical toy systems different synthetic task streams. Ask whether each performs better on the kind of tasks it encountered. Then attack the result: remove the bank, swap the banks, cross-mount them on a deliberately different model, and distil each into text. If divergence is not useful, if portability fails, or if cheap distillation preserves most value, stop talking about a market.

A second gate concerns governance. Can retained state be audited well enough to identify contributors, restrict permitted uses, and honour deletion? If not, the architecture may still be interesting as a private, non-transferable memory system. The market thesis should die. Consultation without transfer may be safer than sale; in many cases, not externalising the memory at all may be safer still.

Machine memories do not need to become property for the question to matter. Systems which learn through relationships force us to decide whether experience can be separated from those who produced it. If the answer is no, that is not an obstacle around which clever engineering should route. It is a result. The most valuable discovery in a market for lived intelligence may be the boundary beyond which intelligence should not become a market.
