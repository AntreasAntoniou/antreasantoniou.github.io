Aircraft Carriers and Fishing Boats

General-purpose models are extraordinary. Deploying one for every tiny decision is still like taking an aircraft carrier fishing.

Developed from a January 2025 long-form research conversation and expanded in the Research Archivum. Edited for clarity and factual precision.

Idea
Most production tasks need surgical precision, not general intelligence.
Mechanism
Distil a broad teacher into local specialists with sharply defined jobs.
Implication
Optimise the system for latency, privacy, reliability, and energy—not parameter count.

Why smaller, specialized models are the future of practical AI

You can use an aircraft carrier to go fishing. Grab a rod, stand on the deck, cast your line. It'll work. But the fuel costs alone would bankrupt you before lunch.

This is essentially what we are doing when we deploy a frontier-scale generalist to classify a timestamp or extract a tag from text. It works, but it spends general-purpose capacity on a narrow decision.

The Scale Obsession

The AI industry has developed a curious fixation with scale. Bigger models, more parameters, longer context windows. The implicit assumption: more is always better.

And for general-purpose reasoning, this holds some truth. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini—these models can write poetry, debug code, and explain quantum mechanics in the same conversation. That generality requires massive parameter counts.

But here's what's often overlooked: most real-world AI tasks don't need general intelligence. They need surgical precision.

The Fishing Boat Approach

In deployment work discussed publicly, I used models below 100 million parameters, each trained for a sharply bounded task.

On tightly specified tasks, a specialist can match or exceed much larger generalist baselines. That is a conditional empirical claim, not a law: the result depends on the data, teacher, task definition, and acceptance test.

The Numbers That Matter

No latency or cost figure is universal: hardware, batching, model size, provider load, and network path all matter. The durable comparison is architectural:

MetricRemote generalistLocal specialist
LatencyNetwork and serving dependentHardware and task dependent
Marginal costUsually usage-pricedNo per-call API fee
PrivacyInput crosses a service boundaryInput can remain on-device
Offline operationUsually unavailableArchitecturally possible

Local inference removes network transit and service queues, but the actual gain must be measured end to end on the target device.

The Warm-Up Effect

Small local models can exhibit a warm-up effect as weights, kernels, and working memory become resident. The size of that effect is runtime- and hardware-dependent, so cold-start and steady-state latency should be reported separately.

When to Use What

This isn't an argument against LLMs. They have their place:

Use LLMs when you need:

  • General reasoning across domains
  • Complex multi-step problem solving
  • Creative generation with broad knowledge
  • Tasks where you can't define the problem precisely

Use distilled micro models when you need:

  • High-throughput classification
  • Real-time processing pipelines
  • Privacy-preserving local inference
  • Specific, well-defined tasks
  • Edge deployment (phones, laptops, IoT)

The key insight: most production AI systems are pipelines of well-defined subtasks. Each subtask is a candidate for a distilled micro model.

The Distillation Process

How do you train a 50-million parameter model to approximate a frontier-scale one on a narrow task?

The answer is distillation. You use the large model as a teacher:

  1. Define your specific task precisely
  2. Generate training data using the teacher model
  3. Train the student on the teacher's outputs
  4. Iterate until the student matches performance

The student learns to mimic the teacher's behavior on that specific task. It doesn't learn general reasoning—it learns the exact skill you need.

The teacher supplies a target distribution, not ground truth. A student can sometimes outperform its teacher on a held-out metric through regularisation, better task data, or evaluation against independent labels. The result has to be measured, not assumed.

The Future is Small

I believe we're entering an era of AI specialization. The generalist models will continue to exist—they're essential for research, exploration, and tasks we can't yet define. But the production workhorses will increasingly be small, fast, and specialized.

The companies that figure out how to efficiently distill large model capabilities into deployable distilled micro models will have a significant advantage. Not in benchmarks or parameter counts, but in the metrics that actually matter: latency, cost, privacy, and reliability.

The aircraft carrier is impressive. But sometimes you just need to catch fish.
