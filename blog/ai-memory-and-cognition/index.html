If You Don’t Use It, You Lose It

What cognitive offloading changes when AI takes over more writing, recall, and mechanical production.

Developed from a January 2025 long-form research conversation and expanded in the Research Archivum. Edited for clarity and factual precision.

Idea
AI does not simply increase productivity; it reallocates which human abilities get exercised.
Mechanism
Use AI aggressively where speed matters, while practising deliberately where retained fluency matters.
Implication
The goal is not to reject offloading, but to choose which cognitive muscles we are willing to lose.

What happens to human capabilities when AI handles more of our thinking?

I've been using AI coding assistants extensively over the past year. Cursor, Copilot, Claude in the terminal. For some tasks, my throughput has increased several-fold—an anecdotal observation, not a controlled productivity measurement.

But I've noticed something uncomfortable: I'm getting worse at writing code from scratch.

Not dramatically worse. Not incompetent. But the muscle memory is fading. The instant recall of syntax, the intuitive pattern-matching, the flow state of implementation—all slightly degraded. I'm becoming better at describing problems and reviewing solutions, worse at the mechanical act of production.

This trade-off might be fine. Maybe even optimal. But it raises a question that goes beyond coding: what happens to human capabilities as AI handles more of our cognitive load?

The Brain's Resource Allocation

The brain is ruthlessly efficient. It doesn't maintain capabilities you don't use. This isn't a design flaw—it's a feature. Neural real estate is expensive. Maintaining unused skills costs metabolic energy and occupies circuits that could be repurposed.

This is why:

  • Languages fade without practice
  • Instruments become harder to play after years away
  • Mathematical fluency declines in careers that don't require it

The principle is simple: if you don't use it, you lose it.

AI tools let us "not use" more and more cognitive functions. Calculators offloaded arithmetic. Search engines offloaded fact recall. GPS offloaded navigation. AI assistants are now offloading writing, reasoning, and analysis.

Each offload is individually rational. Why maintain a skill when a tool does it better? But the aggregate effect is an open question.

The Google Search Effect

Here's a phenomenon that's already well-documented: since the rise of search engines, people have shifted from remembering facts to remembering how to find facts.

During my PhD, I noticed this in myself. I didn't memorize the details of algorithms or the specifics of papers. I memorized the queries that would retrieve them. "MAML meta-learning original paper" is easier to remember than the actual formulation.

This isn't necessarily bad. Query memory is a valid form of knowledge. It's arguably more robust—the facts at the other end of the query can be updated without changing your retrieval strategy.

But it's different. And it changes what you can do in situations where you don't have search access. Or when you need to make creative connections between ideas that wouldn't appear together in search results.

Play as a Training Strategy

I have been struck by the playfulness visible in some Thai martial-arts training: difficult practice can look exploratory rather than punitive. I do not treat that observation as causal evidence about learning.

It does suggest a testable design hypothesis. If practice feels safe enough to explore, people may sustain it longer and sample a wider range of strategies. For AI-displaced skills, enjoyment may therefore be part of retention rather than decoration.

Vibe Coding and Its Discontents

"Vibe coding" is the term for a particular AI-assisted development workflow: you describe what you want in natural language, accept the AI's implementation, test it, iterate with more natural language.

It's shockingly effective for many tasks. But it changes your role from "person who writes code" to "person who describes and reviews code." These are different skills.

The trade-off might be worth it. Someone who ships much faster with AI may be more productive in a particular environment. But they're also more dependent on the AI tooling. And they're developing a different skill set than previous generations of developers.

Is this good or bad? I genuinely don't know. It might just be... change. Different isn't necessarily worse.

The Gamification Hypothesis

If the brain deprioritizes unused skills, and AI gives us reasons to use fewer skills, how do we maintain what we want to maintain?

One answer: make practice intrinsically rewarding.

Duolingo illustrates the motivational difference between a practice loop and a textbook. The skill being trained is the same, but the motivational structure is different. Streaks, points, and silly owl notifications create engagement that pure discipline doesn't.

What if we had the equivalent for AI-displaced skills? Memory games that are actually fun. Coding challenges that feel like puzzles rather than homework. Creative constraints that make "doing it the hard way" enjoyable rather than tedious.

The goal isn't to reject AI tools. They're too useful. The goal is to create parallel structures that keep foundational capabilities alive—not through obligation, but through pleasure.

Education for an AI World

The question becomes acute for education. How do we teach a generation that will have AI assistants from childhood?

The old model—memorise facts, learn procedures, demonstrate competence through recall—is under pressure. The new model... we haven't figured it out yet.

Some principles seem promising:

  • Teach the meta-skill of knowing when to use AI and when to go manual
  • Create experiences of genuine problem-solving that AI can't shortcut
  • Make foundational skills (math, writing, coding) enjoyable enough to practice
  • Develop judgment about when AI outputs are trustworthy

But we're all figuring this out in real time. The students starting school today will enter a workforce we can barely imagine.

What I'm Actually Doing

For myself, I've settled on a hybrid approach:

  1. Use AI aggressively for production work where speed matters
  2. Practice fundamentals deliberately through side projects and puzzles
  3. Stay curious about the atrophy and monitor which skills feel rusty
  4. Accept some decline in areas where the trade-off is worth it

I'm worse at raw code production than I was two years ago. I'm better at system design, problem decomposition, and reviewing AI outputs. The portfolio of skills has shifted.

Whether this is optimal depends on assumptions about the future that I can't verify. If AI capabilities plateau, maybe I should have maintained more traditional skills. If they accelerate, maybe I didn't lean in hard enough.

All I can do is stay adaptive. The brain will reallocate either way—I might as well be intentional about the direction.
