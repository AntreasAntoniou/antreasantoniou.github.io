Attention Learns Its Own Wiring

Attention as dynamic connectivity: a model choosing which relationships matter for this input.

Developed from a January 2025 long-form research conversation and expanded in the Research Archivum. Edited for clarity and factual precision.

Idea
Architecture is a communication graph, and fixed graphs bake assumptions into what a network can compute efficiently.
Mechanism
Queries and keys make the effective connectivity pattern conditional on the input.
Implication
Topology becomes a learnable computational resource—and therefore a candidate scaling axis.

How transformers learn to focus on what matters

The 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need" made a bold claim in its title. You don't need convolutions. You don't need recurrence. Just attention.

But what is attention, actually? And why does it work so well that it's now a foundation of the dominant family of large AI models?

The Connectivity Problem

Neural networks are, at their core, connection patterns between neurons. How you wire them up determines what they can learn.

Before transformers, different tasks required different wiring:

Convolutional Neural Networks used local connections. Each neuron only looked at a small patch of its input—a 3×3 region of an image, for example. This encodes the assumption that nearby pixels are related, which is true for images.

Recurrent Neural Networks used sequential connections. Each neuron received input from the previous time step, plus the current input. This encodes the assumption that sequences have temporal dependencies, which is true for language.

Both approaches bake assumptions into the architecture. The connectivity pattern is fixed at design time based on human intuitions about the data.

What If the Network Could Choose?

Attention asks a different question: what if we let the network decide which connections matter?

Instead of fixing connectivity patterns, attention computes them dynamically based on the input. Every element can potentially attend to every other element. The network learns which relationships are important.

Here's the key formula, simplified:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q × K^T) × V

In plain English:

  1. For each element, compute how much it should attend to every other element (the Q×K^T part)
  2. Normalize these attention scores (the softmax)
  3. Use the scores to weight a combination of values (× V)

The Q (query), K (key), and V (value) are all learned transformations of the input. The network learns what to look for (queries), how to be found (keys), and what information to contribute (values).

Why This Is Revolutionary

Consider a sentence: "The animal didn't cross the road because it was too tired."

What does "it" refer to? The animal, obviously. But this requires understanding that "tired" applies to animate things, and the animal is the only animate thing in the sentence.

In an RNN, information flows sequentially. By the time you reach "it", you've processed "animal" many steps ago. The connection is indirect.

In an attention-based model, "it" can directly attend to "animal". The model learns that pronouns should attend strongly to their antecedents. The connection is immediate, regardless of position.

This directness is why attention handles long-range dependencies better than RNNs. Information doesn't have to flow through a chain of sequential steps—it can jump directly to where it's needed.

Self-Attention: Attending to Yourself

The "self" in self-attention means the input attends to itself. Every position in the input can look at every other position.

This is computationally expensive—O(n²) in sequence length—but extraordinarily flexible. The network can learn dense, input-dependent connectivity patterns within its parameterisation: long-range, short-range, and mixtures of both.

Convolutions privilege locality and recurrence privileges sequential state. Self-attention offers a broader communication pattern, but it is not a universal learner and still carries architectural assumptions.

Multi-Head Attention

One attention pattern might not be enough. Maybe you want to attend to both syntactic and semantic relationships. Or both local and global context.

Multi-head attention runs several attention computations in parallel, each with different learned parameters. The outputs are concatenated and combined.

This lets the model capture multiple types of relationships simultaneously. One head might learn to track subjects and verbs. Another might track sentiment words. Another might track document structure.

The Trade-offs

Attention isn't free:

Quadratic complexity: O(n²) means long sequences are expensive. This is why context windows exist.

Position agnosticism: Pure attention has no notion of order. You need to add positional encodings separately.

Memory-intensive: Storing attention matrices for long sequences uses significant memory.

These trade-offs explain ongoing research into sparse attention, linear attention, and hybrid architectures. The core attention insight is powerful, but the specific implementation has room for improvement.

Why It Won

Despite the trade-offs, attention has become the dominant paradigm because of one key property: it learns.

Fixed architectures encode human assumptions. Those assumptions might be wrong, or incomplete, or only applicable to certain data.

Attention learns connectivity from the data, reducing the amount fixed by the designer. Positional schemes, tokenisation, depth, and other human choices still matter.

This flexibility helps explain why related attention architectures work across text, images, audio, and video.

The wiring that matters emerges from training. That's the real revolution.
