A field manual, not a commandment tablet

Writing advice

Academic writing did not improve for me by osmosis. During my PhD—especially the first year—I treated it as a skill that needed deliberate practice. These are the things that changed how I wrote.

Antreas Antoniou · learned through mini-papers, teaching, supervision, and a great deal of being edited

→

Before writing any section

What does this section need the reader to believe or understand?

Write the answer in one sentence. Make every paragraph serve it. If a paragraph does not, remove it—or admit that the point has changed.

How I trained it

Writing gets better through active practice

Writing papers is necessary, but it is a painfully slow training loop. I improved faster by creating shorter loops: write, explain, critique, compare, repeat.

Compress

Write two-page mini-papers

Every two weeks, I wrote a two-page mini-paper on whatever I was thinking about. Two pages force a choice: what is the point, and what can safely disappear?

Explain

Teach difficult ideas

I spent a good five hours of many weeks writing for students with very different backgrounds. Explaining the same machinery to different audiences exposes every place where your own understanding is still hand-wavy.

Diagnose

Read student reports

Reading student reports taught me a great deal about what not to do, and occasionally gave me a beautiful example of what to do. Structural problems are much easier to see when they are not yours.

Critique

Read four new papers every week

I sourced papers from arXiv-sanity, critiqued the writing—not only the research—and compared notes with peers. Reading without judging the writing is a missed training signal.

Collect

Build a shelf of exemplars

I collected exemplars of well-written papers through daily reading and community collections, including this list of well-written machine-learning papers. “Good writing” becomes useful when you can point to an example and say why it works.

Borrow

Learn from people who have survived it

Andrej Karpathy's PhD Survival Guide—especially the paper-writing section—helped me connect the mechanics of writing to the stranger project of surviving a PhD.

The one-point test

Writing is the same problem at several scales

A document can have subpoints, but they must carry the main point. The same discipline repeats inside every section, paragraph, sentence, figure, and table.

Document

One contribution. A reader should be able to state it in one sentence.

Section

One job. The first paragraph makes that job visible; everything else earns its place by supporting it.

Paragraph

One move. Point first, support in the middle, then a conclusion or a clean hand-off to what comes next.

Sentence

One tractable thought. Put known information early and new information later. Give it a clear subject and verb.

This structural discipline is distilled from Amos Storkey's BayesWatch Writing Rules. The page originally lived at https://www.bayeswatch.com/unlisted/ and now has a permanent home at /writing/.

Before another human has to read it

Make the reader's job easier

Precision is not ornamental. It is how you prevent the reader from having to reconstruct the experiment, the argument, or the object you meant.

?

Define before use

Introduce a concept before asking it to carry another concept. Use one term for one thing and keep using it.

()

Supply the missing arguments

Normalisation of what, against what, and why? Average over which quantity, population, or axis? English terms take arguments too.

≠

Keep conceptual types intact

A representation is not the represented object. A set is not an element of that set. Academic English is typed; do not make the reader debug a type error.

↳

Justify assertions

Every claim needs support from prior work, current evidence, or a preceding argument. If the support is missing during drafting, leave a marker and repair it.

✂

Split long sentences

A long sentence often contains several claims competing for one verb. Split it. If a detail survives nowhere else, ask whether it mattered.

◎

Give every figure one job

Reference it in the text. Make the caption self-contained. State the conclusion you want the reader to test against the evidence.

A caption is a tiny argument

Do not merely describe what is visible

A useful caption lets the reader understand the evidence without hunting through the prose. It should also expose the limitation that changes how the figure can be interpreted.

  1. Question What question does the figure answer?
  2. Material What exactly is shown?
  3. Conditions Which conditions and controls are included?
  4. Measurement What quantity, method, or software produced it?
  5. Conclusion What should the reader take forward?
  6. Boundary What limitation matters for interpretation?

The two-minute pass

Before you send the draft

  • Can you state the point in one sentence?
  • Does the first paragraph reveal that point?
  • Does every paragraph support it?
  • Are technical terms defined before use?
  • Does every measured claim name the measured quantity?
  • Does each figure caption state a conclusion and a limitation?
  • Is anything present only because it happened?

Write to be understood. Then make it worth understanding.
