Why I Don’t Regret My PhD

I did not buy a credential. I found a way to be paid, modestly, for sustained tinkering.

Adapted from a 2025 conversation and developed in the Research Archivum. This is a personal account, not general career advice; field conditions and incentives are stated as dated context rather than permanent truths.

Choice
Use doctoral training as protected time for deep exploration.
Mechanism
Repeated experiments turn uncertainty and failure into research judgement.
Boundary
The bargain only works if the years themselves are worth living.

“Do you regret doing your PhD?” is rarely a neutral question. It normally arrives carrying exhibits for the prosecution: low pay, delayed earnings, broken academic labour markets, a thesis read by six people if you include your parents, and the friend who joined industry and now owns furniture that did not come flat-packed.

The case is not frivolous. A PhD can be an atrocious bargain. It can trap someone in a topic they no longer love, under poor supervision, pursuing a credential that the work they actually want does not require. I know people, inside and outside machine learning, who regret the years. Their experience should not be argued away by someone else's happier story.

But I do not regret mine. The reason is not that every hardship secretly built character, or that the letters after my name guaranteed a career. I entered with a simpler bargain in mind: I wanted time to tinker deeply with intelligent systems. A scholarship offered a way for someone to pay me—very modestly—to do that with structure, peers, and accountability. It was a low-paid passion project that could, if I organised the tinkering well enough, produce a doctorate.

The future-tech shortlist

Near the end of my undergraduate engineering degree, I made the sort of list an earnest engineer makes when trying to choose a life: quantum computing, artificial intelligence, nanotechnology, genetic engineering, renewable energy. Which technologies excited me, and which might change the world?

Then I encountered a talk about deep learning and the 2012 ImageNet result. A neural network had cut the top-5 classification error dramatically on a difficult image benchmark. What struck me was not merely the score. It was the transfer of labour. Instead of a researcher defining every useful visual feature—this edge, that texture, this carefully engineered descriptor—the system learned layered representations from data.

At the time I was working on drones. I walked into my supervisor’s office with the calm, measured proposal: “You know those drones we were working on? We’re not doing drones anymore. We’re doing deep learning.” Fortunately, he believed enough in the idea, or in my enthusiasm, to let me pivot my dissertation.

That episode contains the first reason I value the path. Curiosity was allowed to alter the plan. Competitions such as Kaggle and early deep-learning libraries gave me ways to learn by building, not merely by admiring papers from a distance. The PhD later expanded that tinkering into a discipline.

Paid tinkering is still work

Calling a PhD “paid tinkering” can sound like four years in a workshop, joyfully attaching neural networks to things until a thesis falls out. The reality includes failed runs, debugging, reading, writing, teaching, deadlines, reviews, and the administrative side quest that appears whenever the main quest becomes interesting.

The word tinkering does not mean random activity. Good research tinkering is structured. You form a hypothesis, build the smallest experiment that can distinguish it from an alternative, inspect the failure, and decide what the result permits you to believe. You keep enough notes that future-you does not repeat the same “new” idea three months later. Eventually the exploration becomes a contribution another person can evaluate and reproduce.

My programme at the University of Edinburgh was an integrated master’s-plus-PhD programme with cohort-based doctoral training. That context mattered. I was not alone in a room attempting to derive a research identity from first principles. I had peers, teachers, opportunities to teach, and enough time for intuitions to compound. The qualification was an output of that process; it was not the only product.

Learning to bungee-jump

Research repeatedly brings you to an edge where the map stops. I describe it as bungee-jumping into the abyss. At first, every jump feels like a referendum on whether you belong. The experiment fails; therefore perhaps you are not clever enough. The paper is rejected; therefore perhaps the work is worthless. Months disappear into an approach that produces a flat line.

With enough practice, failure changes category. It becomes information. That hypothesis does not survive this test. That implementation confounded two variables. That attractive direction needs a resource you do not have. You learn to choose experiments that fail cheaply and reveal something specific. You learn when to make one more attempt and when “one more tweak” is fear wearing a lab coat.

This is what I mean by failing optimally. It is not celebrating waste or lowering standards. It is maximising information per unit of time while working in uncertainty. The emotional skill and the technical skill reinforce each other: clearer kill criteria make failure less personal; less fear makes it easier to admit what the data says.

I use that training constantly. Research roles demand it, but so do startups, product decisions, and any project where the answer is not sitting at the back of the textbook. The PhD gave me repeated, supervised exposure to not knowing.

The bargain is field- and person-dependent

In the 2025 source conversation, I said that a PhD still made sense for high-level AI research. That was a description of the path I knew and of hiring patterns I had observed—not a universal prerequisite. Some research roles strongly prefer doctoral training. Some outstanding researchers develop equivalent depth through industry labs, open research, engineering practice, or unusual combinations of all three. Titles and filters change.

The opportunity cost also changes by field, country, funding package, supervisor, and personal circumstance. Five years of modest income is not a quirky aesthetic choice if you support a family or carry debt. A prestigious programme does not redeem abusive supervision. A hot field can cool down before a thesis is submitted. Conversely, a subject without an obvious industry premium may still be worth studying if the intellectual life itself is the point and the finances are genuinely workable.

There is a counterfactual I cannot run. I do not know who I would be had I spent those years in a company. Perhaps I would have learned faster in some dimensions, shipped more, earned more, and reached similar work without the doctorate. Personal essays often compare the life lived with an imaginary alternative designed to lose. I will not do that.

Ask about Tuesday afternoon

If someone asks me whether they should do a PhD, I would not begin with the title they want five years later. I would ask what they want to do on an ordinary Tuesday afternoon. Read papers? Design experiments? Discover that yesterday's run used the wrong data split? Explain an idea to students? Rewrite an argument until it finally says what the evidence supports?

If those activities are merely toll booths on the road to a credential, the road is too long. If the activities themselves feel like a life you want—and the funding, supervisor, and exit options withstand serious scrutiny—the doctorate can be a rare piece of protected time. Not protected from pressure, and certainly not protected from failure, but protected enough to go deep.

I do not regret my PhD because I was doing what I wanted to do anyway: trying to understand learning systems, building things, teaching what I had understood, and jumping repeatedly into uncertain territory. Someone paid me poorly to practise. The lasting return was not certainty. It was becoming less frightened by the absence of it.
