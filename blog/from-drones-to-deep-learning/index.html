From Drones to Deep Learning

The career-changing skill was not predicting the future. It was recognising when new evidence made my existing plan the expensive option.

Adapted from a 2025 conversation and developed in the Research Archivum. Personal details are limited to the account in that source; this is a reconstruction of a decision, not a universal career recipe.

Observation
The ImageNet result looked discontinuous to me.
Mechanism
A standing technology radar made the signal actionable instead of merely impressive.
Implication
Revisit commitments when evidence changes the opportunity cost—not whenever hype gets loud.

I walked into my supervisor's office with a proposal that was admirably concise and professionally inconvenient: we were not doing drones anymore. We were doing deep learning.

Until then I had been working on drones, including computer vision for navigation. The change was not caused by a lifelong certainty that artificial intelligence would win, nor by an exquisitely timed spreadsheet. I had seen a talk about deep learning and the 2012 ImageNet result. The AlexNet paper reported its seven-CNN ensemble at 15.3% top-5 test error, against 26.2% for the strongest listed non-neural entry—a gap of 10.9 percentage points, or roughly a 42% relative reduction in error. I did not yet understand the whole mechanism. I understood that the trajectory had bent.

My supervisor believed in the change, and I moved into deep learning for my dissertation. Competitions such as Kaggle and early tools such as Keras then gave me ways to learn by building. That decision helped put me on the path into machine-learning research. It is tempting, from the far side of a decision that worked, to turn it into a heroic origin myth. The more useful story is less flattering: I had prepared a rough map, received a strong signal, and was willing to admit that my current project no longer looked like the best use of my time.

The map came before the signal

As I was finishing my engineering degree, I considered several technologies that might have a large impact: quantum computing, artificial intelligence, nanotechnology, genetic engineering, and energy generation. This was not a forecast with probabilities calibrated to three decimal places. It was a technology radar—a list of domains worth watching closely enough that a real change would be legible.

The radar mattered because novelty is otherwise just weather. Every week produces a dramatic demo, an alarming headline, and a founder announcing that history began on Tuesday. If you have no prior model of a field, intensity is easy to mistake for information. I had already identified AI as a high-potential area, so the ImageNet result landed in context.

I was looking for a particular combination: advanced enough to produce evidence, young enough that basic questions remained open, and accessible enough that I could actually participate. A fascinating technology can still be the wrong personal bet if progress depends on infrastructure, regulation, or expertise you cannot reach. “Will this matter?” and “Can I contribute now?” are separate questions.

What looked discontinuous

Before deep learning's rise in computer vision, a great deal of effort went into hand-designed features: ways to tell a system that edges, corners, shapes, or other human-selected patterns might matter. The result described in the talk suggested a different allocation of labour. Given data and the right training setup, a neural network could learn useful representations rather than requiring every visual feature to be specified in advance.

The important observation was not simply “one benchmark score is higher”. It was that the method appeared to move work from hand-crafting task-specific features toward designing systems that learned those features. If that transfer generalised, it would change what researchers spent their time doing. The result therefore carried both magnitude and mechanism.

I also saw a path to testing it myself. Public competitions supplied problems and feedback. Keras lowered the friction of constructing models. This matters when judging a wave: a result is more actionable when tools, data, and a community allow independent people to reproduce, challenge, and extend it. A spectacular closed demonstration may be important, but it offers a different kind of career opportunity.

Changing direction is an option calculation

The common story says that pivoting means courageously ignoring sunk costs. Sunk costs are indeed sunk, but present capabilities are not worthless. Work on drones had given me engineering context and a concrete reason to care about perception. The question was not whether to throw the past into a ceremonial fire. It was whether another direction now offered a better learning gradient.

I think of the decision as comparing options. Staying preserved momentum, an existing project, and expectations. Switching offered access to a rapidly opening research area but introduced uncertainty and the risk that the apparent discontinuity was noise. The ImageNet result altered that balance enough for me to act.

This framing also explains why “pivot quickly” is bad general advice. If every impressive paper makes you abandon your work, you do not become adaptive; you become pollen. Expertise needs uninterrupted time. The threshold for switching should be high enough that novelty alone cannot trigger it, and low enough that identity does not trap you after the evidence changes.

The survivorship problem

There was luck. I encountered the talk. My supervisor was open to the proposal. The field continued to develop rather than freezing after one result. Many people make thoughtful bets on plausible technologies that mature slowly, move in another direction, or never become accessible. Their decisions do not become conference-keynote anecdotes.

Looking backward also makes signals appear cleaner than they were. Community energy can indicate a genuine opening, but it can also produce a bubble. Unexpected performance can reveal a new mechanism, but it can also depend on a benchmark quirk. Skeptics changing their minds is informative only if they changed because of evidence, not because funding and status moved first.

A decision process should therefore contain a disconfirmation test. What result would make the breakthrough look narrower? Can independent teams reproduce it? Does the advantage survive outside one dataset? Is the underlying resource requirement falling or concentrating? Can I run a small experiment before rebuilding my identity around it? In my case, competitions and practical tools allowed that exploration.

A radar needs maintenance, not worship

In the 2025 conversation, I named local AI, embodied AI, biological interfaces, and artificial life as areas I was watching. That was a dated watchlist, not a declaration that any of them had become “the next ImageNet”. Predictions should be stored with timestamps because memory is an unscrupulous editor: it keeps the guesses that worked and quietly drops the rest.

A useful radar records observations separately from mechanisms and predictions. Observation: a measured capability changed. Mechanism: a new representation, data regime, or engineering constraint might explain the change. Prediction: if the mechanism generalises, particular costs or behaviours should improve. Then revisit the prediction. Otherwise trend-watching becomes fan fiction with charts.

The same method applies at a smaller scale than choosing a research field. A team can ask whether a new tool changes the cost of an experiment. A student can notice that a previously inaccessible area now has open datasets. An engineer can keep a stable core of expertise while reserving time for cheap probes at the edges. You do not need to resign every time the radar beeps.

The wave is evidence changing the price of waiting

My move from drones to deep learning was decisive, but the decision did not require certainty. Certainty would have arrived after the interesting part of the opportunity. What I needed was a prior map, a discontinuous result with a plausible mechanism, an accessible way to test it, and permission—from my supervisor and from myself—to update.

The closing implication is not “follow the energy” in the motivational-poster sense. Crowds are energetic in casinos. It is to build enough understanding before the breakthrough that you can tell when evidence changes the price of waiting. Then run a probe, expose the decision to failure, and preserve what your previous work taught you.

I did not know exactly what deep learning would become when I walked into that office. I knew the old plan had been priced under yesterday's evidence. Sometimes the most conservative thing you can do is continue because changing direction feels dramatic. Sometimes the rational move is to put the drone down, open the new tools, and find out whether the wave can carry your weight.
