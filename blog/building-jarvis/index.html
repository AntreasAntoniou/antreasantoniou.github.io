Building Jarvis

The engineering path from stateless chatbots to personal AI with durable, private context.

Developed from a January 2025 long-form research conversation and expanded in the Research Archivum. Edited for clarity and factual precision.

Idea
Without memory, an AI assistant is a clever stranger at the start of every conversation.
Mechanism
Capture, structure, retrieve, synthesise, then earn the right to become proactive.
Implication
Persistent context turns isolated prompts into collaboration—but only if the memory remains under user control.

What it would actually mean to have an AI that knows you

When people ask what kind of personal AI I want to build, I can give the technical answer: small models, long-term memory pipelines, and local inference.

The simpler answer is: Jarvis.

Not literally the Marvel character. But the concept—an AI assistant that actually knows you, remembers your context, and helps in ways that generic chatbots never could.

The Dream Query

Here's the kind of interaction I want to have:

"Hey, remember that thing we discussed last Tuesday? I was talking to John, and there was that paper he mentioned. I think we were at the coffee shop. What was it called?"

A useful AI assistant should handle this. It has the context—it was there (metaphorically) when the conversation happened. It should be able to answer: "You mean the attention mechanism paper? John recommended 'Attention Is All You Need' during your 3pm coffee meeting. He said it was foundational for understanding transformer architectures."

Most current assistants cannot do this reliably across the full context of a person’s life. Product memory is improving, but durable, inspectable, user-controlled recall across devices and years remains unsolved.

Why Memory Is The Killer Feature

Without memory, AI assistants are essentially clever strangers. Every conversation starts from zero. You have to re-explain context, re-establish who you are, re-describe what you're working on.

This limits AI assistance to tasks that can be fully specified in a single prompt. Need help editing a document? Fine—paste the document. Need help with a project that spans months, involves dozens of people, and has accumulated context across hundreds of conversations? You're on your own, because the AI can't see any of that.

Memory changes everything:

Contextual understanding: The AI knows what projects you're working on, who your collaborators are, what decisions you've made, what problems you've encountered.

Accumulated knowledge: Instead of asking "what's the status?" you ask "what changed since yesterday?" The AI tracks the delta.

Personalization: The AI learns your preferences, your writing style, your workflow. Suggestions become increasingly relevant.

Proactive assistance: With enough context, the AI can anticipate needs rather than just respond to requests.

The Automatic Documentation Dream

One thing I deeply want: documentation that writes itself.

Every time I build something, there's a moment at the end where I think: "I should document how this works." And then I don't, because documentation is tedious and I've already moved on mentally.

An AI that's been watching my process should be able to generate that documentation automatically. It saw what I did. It can write down the steps, the decisions, the gotchas. Not a perfect document, but a first draft that captures 80% of what matters.

The same applies to:

  • Standup reports: "What did I do yesterday?" should be automatically answerable.
  • Meeting summaries: The AI was present (via screen and audio capture). It knows what was discussed.
  • Time tracking: Where did the hours actually go? The AI knows.
  • Handoff documents: Bringing someone else up to speed on a project becomes trivial if the AI has full context.

The Gap Between Vision and Reality

We're not there yet. Current "AI assistants" are mostly chatbots with delusions of helpfulness.

The technical challenges are real:

The memory problem: Storing and retrieving relevant context from potentially millions of events is hard. This is the needle-in-haystack challenge—current architectures aren't great at it.

Privacy constraints: For an AI to have this level of context, it needs to observe your life. That's a massive privacy surface. The implementation needs to be trustworthy.

Compute requirements: Running inference continuously, processing screen content, managing embeddings for retrieval—this takes resources. It needs to run locally to preserve privacy, which limits what's feasible.

UX design: How do you interact with an AI that knows this much about you? The conversational interfaces we have now feel inadequate.

The Incremental Path

We are not going to wake up one day with Jarvis. The engineering path is incremental.

Step 1 is memory. Can we capture what you're doing and make it retrievable? LTM (Long-Term Memory) systems are this foundation.

Step 2 is understanding. Can we process that raw capture into meaningful narratives? OCR, activity classification, context extraction.

Step 3 is retrieval. Can we find the right information when you need it? Embedding-based search, structured queries, relevance ranking.

Step 4 is synthesis. Can we combine retrieved context with reasoning to actually help? This is where the LLM component matters.

Step 5 is proactivity. Can we anticipate needs rather than just responding? Pattern recognition over historical behavior, prediction of routine tasks.

Each step unlocks new capabilities. Each step is hard.

Why Local Matters

All of this needs to run locally—or at least, the memory and sensitive processing parts do.

I've wanted a "follow me around and remember everything" tool for years. The reason I never used the cloud-based ones that exist: I don't trust them with that level of access to my life.

The controversy around always-on screen capture showed that this concern is not abstract. Personal memory systems concentrate sensitive data, so local processing, encryption, deletion, and access control are core requirements.

For Jarvis to work, it needs to be your Jarvis. Running on your hardware, storing data on your devices, sending nothing to the cloud unless you explicitly choose to. The moment it phones home, trust evaporates.

The 10-Year Vision

If you ask me what AI looks like in 10 years, this is the core of my answer: truly personal AI assistants that have earned the right to know everything about you because they've proven they can be trusted.

Not just chatbots you talk to. Persistent entities that accumulate context over years. That remember not just what you said, but what you saw, heard, read, wrote, built.

Some of this might be embodied. Imagine a small device—something unobtrusive, maybe wearable—that's always with you, always listening (locally), always building your external memory.

Some of it might be ambient. AI embedded in your environment, in your tools, in the software you use daily.

The key shift: from AI as a tool you use occasionally to AI as a partner that's always present, always accumulating context, always ready to help.

We're building toward that. Every distilled micro model, every memory optimization, every privacy feature—it's all in service of that vision.

Jarvis isn't science fiction. It's just engineering we haven't finished yet.
