Distillation: Teaching Small Models Big Tricks

How task-specific students turn broad model capability into fast, local inference—and what gets lost.

Developed from a January 2025 long-form research conversation and expanded in the Research Archivum. Edited for clarity and factual precision.

Idea
A large model can be a capability frontier without being the model that ships.
Mechanism
Generate and curate task-specific supervision, then train the smallest student that clears the real acceptance tests.
Implication
Frontier research and efficient deployment become complementary layers of one system.

How to compress frontier-scale capabilities into models that run on your laptop

Here's a puzzle: we have frontier-scale language models with remarkably broad capabilities. But they require data centers to run, cost money per query, and can't work offline.

Meanwhile, we have small models that run locally in milliseconds. But they're not smart enough for most useful tasks.

What if you could transfer the intelligence from the big model to the small one?

This is knowledge distillation. And it's the key technique behind practical AI deployment.

The Teacher-Student Paradigm

The concept is simple. You have two models:

The Teacher: A massive, powerful model. It might have hundreds of billions of parameters. It runs in the cloud, costs real money, and takes seconds to respond. But it's extremely capable.

The Student: A tiny model. Maybe 50-100 million parameters. It runs locally in milliseconds. But it needs to be trained.

The distillation process: use the teacher to generate training data for the student.

Why This Works

Traditional machine learning requires labeled data. If you want a model that classifies sentiment, you need millions of examples labeled "positive" or "negative" by humans.

But labeling is expensive. Humans are slow, inconsistent, and don't scale.

Large language models changed this equation by making synthetic annotation cheap and scalable. Their labels are not automatically correct or more consistent than human labels: they require validation, disagreement analysis, and targeted human review.

The teacher becomes one source of supervision, not an oracle.

The Training Loop

Here's how distillation typically works:

  1. Define your task precisely. Not "general language understanding" but something specific: "Given a user query, classify the time reference as past/present/future."

  2. Generate diverse inputs. This might be real data from your application, or synthetic data designed to cover edge cases.

  3. Run inputs through the teacher. Get the teacher's outputs for each input. These become your labels.

  4. Train the student on teacher outputs. The student learns to mimic the teacher's behavior on this specific task.

  5. Iterate. Find cases where the student fails, add them to training data, retrain.

The result: a small model that performs like the teacher—but only on this specific task.

The Teacher Is Not Ground Truth

A student trained only to imitate teacher outputs inherits teacher errors. But “cannot exceed the teacher” is too strong: with independent ground-truth labels, regularisation, better task data, or a narrower evaluation distribution, a student can outperform its teacher on the measured task.

The honest procedure is to evaluate both against an external acceptance set. Teacher agreement is a training signal; it is not the definition of correctness.

What You Lose and What You Keep

Distillation preserves task-specific behavior. It does not preserve general capabilities.

A frontier-scale generalist can:

  • Answer questions about history
  • Write poetry
  • Debug code
  • Explain quantum mechanics
  • And do your specific task

A distilled 100-million-parameter model can:

  • Do your specific task

That's the trade-off. The student becomes a specialist. It loses the generalist capabilities of the teacher.

This is actually desirable for deployment. You don't want your timestamp classifier to have opinions about poetry. You want it to classify timestamps—fast, cheap, and accurately.

Practical Considerations

Data quality matters more than data quantity. A smaller dataset with high-quality teacher labels often beats a massive dataset with noise. Curate your inputs carefully.

Task definition is critical. Fuzzy tasks produce fuzzy models. The more precisely you can specify what you want, the better the student will learn it.

Multiple teachers can help. For some tasks, labels from multiple strong models can be reconciled or ensembled. This can smooth out individual model quirks.

Iteration is key. First distillation attempts usually reveal gaps. Find the failure modes, add targeted training data, repeat.

The Compression Ratio

How much can you compress? It depends on the task.

For narrow, well-defined tasks, compression can be substantial. The ratio is not meaningful without the task, dataset, baseline, hardware, and acceptance threshold, so those belong beside any reported number.

For broader tasks, compression is more limited. There's a minimum complexity required to capture the behavior you want.

The art is finding where your task falls on this spectrum and sizing your student model appropriately.

The Deployment Advantage

Once you have a distilled model, deployment is transformed:

MetricRemote teacherLocal student
LatencyNetwork and serving dependentDevice and task dependent
Marginal costTypically usage-pricedNo per-call API charge
PrivacyInput crosses a service boundaryInput can remain local
ThroughputService limitsHardware limits

For high-volume systems, distillation can shift cost from repeated remote inference toward training, validation, and local operation. It does not make inference free.

The Path Forward

Distillation is one route by which AI becomes practical. The massive models are research tools and capability frontiers. The distilled models are what actually runs in products.

Every time a new capability emerges in large models, the question becomes: can we distill this? If yes, it may be deployable locally; if no, the capability may still require a larger model or a different system design.

This is why both tracks of development matter. We need the frontier models to push capabilities. And we need the distillation techniques to make those capabilities deployable.

The teacher learns new tricks. The student brings them to the real world.
