When Does an Architecture Become a Learner?

A hypothesis about the convnet–transformer crossover, fast weights, and whether meta-learning can move the boundary.

This is a live research hypothesis developed from my work on meta-learning and architecture search. The causal link proposed here has not yet been established experimentally.

Observation
ConvNets can lead without pretraining at low data; transformers can overtake after sufficient data consumption.
Hypothesis
Pretraining may teach attention an input- or episode-conditioned inner learning procedure.
Test
Give convolution a matched adaptation channel and measure whether the crossover moves or disappears.

Here is a question I cannot stop poking at: what if transformers eventually beat convolutional networks not because attention is simply a better static architecture, but because enough pretraining teaches attention how to learn inside its own forward pass?

Take a vision transformer and a convnet. Train both from scratch with little data and the convnet will often embarrass the transformer. Locality and translation equivariance are extremely useful priors for images; convolution receives them before seeing a single example. Give the transformer enough data, regularisation and pretraining, however, and the ordering can reverse.

The reversal can survive into few-shot transfer, but “few-shot” is doing some criminal accounting here. The downstream learner might see five labelled examples per class. The representation may already have consumed millions—or hundreds of millions—of examples before those five arrived.

The usual explanation is inductive bias. A convnet gets useful structure for free. A transformer begins with a weaker spatial prior and must buy the structure with data. I think this is probably part of the answer.

I wonder whether the transformer is buying something else as well.

The part of attention that behaves like a learner

My first rough version of this thought was that the query, key and value matrices behave like a hypernetwork. That is too sloppy.

The matrices WQ, WK and WV are ordinary learned parameters—the slow weights. Given an input X, they produce queries, keys and values:

Q = XWQ      K = XWK      V = XWV

Attention then computes a mixing operator from the current input:

A(X) = softmax(QKᵀ / √d)      Y = A(X)V

The interesting object is A(X). The slow weights produce a different communication graph for every input. These words should exchange information in this sentence; those image patches should exchange information in that image. The architecture is fixed, but part of the effective computation is written on demand.

In linear attention, the interpretation becomes especially clean:

Y = φ(Q) [φ(K)ᵀV]

The bracketed term is constructed from the current context and acts like an ephemeral fast-weight matrix. This is not merely a metaphor invented for this post: linearised attention has been formally connected to fast-weight programmers, and small transformers trained over task distributions can learn gradient-descent-like algorithms in their forward pass.

A standard convolution behaves differently. Its activations obviously depend on the input, but the interaction rule is fixed: the same learned kernel slides across the image. It cannot decide that two distant regions should suddenly exchange information unless we give it the depth, receptive field or explicit machinery to do so. Dynamic convolutions, conditional filters, FiLM, hypernetworks and gradient-based inner loops can provide that machinery.

Maybe a transformer is a slow programme that learns to write a temporary learner.

Pretraining as the outer loop

Meta-learning normally has two timescales. An outer loop sees many tasks and learns the conditions for rapid adaptation. An inner loop uses a small amount of task-specific evidence to produce a useful task-specific predictor.

Now look at transformer pretraining through that lens. The slow parameters are updated across an enormous stream of examples, contexts and latent tasks. Inside each forward pass, attention constructs input-dependent routes and temporary associations. Under a sufficiently rich training distribution, those routes could stop being merely flexible feature mixing and become a learned adaptation procedure.

This suggests a different explanation for the data crossover. Early in training, the transformer's weak built-in spatial prior is simply a disadvantage. The convnet knows something useful about images immediately. Later, once pretraining has exposed the transformer to enough variation, its capacity to construct conditional computation may become more useful than the fixed prior.

The crossover would mark a change in what the architecture has learned to do, not just how good its features have become.

That is the hypothesis. It is not yet a result.

The convnet is not forbidden from learning to learn

If the explanation is correct, then the comparison between a conventionally trained convnet and a heavily pretrained transformer is incomplete. We have given one architecture a vast outer loop and an easy path to data-conditioned computation, then asked why it adapts well.

The useful question is not whether a convnet can magically become a transformer. It is whether giving convolution a matched adaptation channel changes where the ordering flips.

I would compare at least five systems:

  1. a static convnet;
  2. a static vision transformer;
  3. a MAML++ convnet with explicit gradient-based adaptation;
  4. a support-conditioned convnet whose filters or feature modulation are generated dynamically;
  5. an episodic transformer which can process the support examples and query together.

Train them under both flat and episodic objectives, across logarithmically increasing data budgets. Match parameters, training examples, optimiser steps, augmentation and compute as closely as the architectures allow. During meta-learning, permute the label meanings independently in every episode so nobody can pass by memorising a fixed class decoder.

Then stop asking only which model wins. Estimate the data scale at which the expected ordering changes.

If explicit meta-learning or dynamic support conditioning moves the convnet–transformer crossover towards larger data, shrinks it, or removes it, then attention's advantage may partly be an adaptation advantage. If the crossover barely moves, the inner-learner story is probably not doing much causal work. Global receptive fields, representation geometry, optimisation, regularisation or other architectural details become better explanations.

This is the nice kind of idea: the experiment contains a fairly direct way to kill it.

The annoying caveat

There is a problem hiding inside the words “few-shot transformer.” A conventional vision transformer normally processes each image independently. If the support set and query never share a forward pass, the model cannot infer the episode's arbitrary label mapping through attention. It has no access to the little task we are claiming it learns.

Its attention can still adapt computation within an image. It can change which patches exchange information according to the image content. But within-example conditional routing is not the same thing as episode-level meta-learning.

This distinction matters because a pretrained ViT may dominate few-shot transfer simply by producing better frozen representations. A nearest-centroid or linear classifier can exploit those representations without the backbone learning anything new from the support examples.

So the experiment must separate four things that are often blended together:

  • static representation transfer;
  • conditional computation within an example;
  • feed-forward adaptation conditioned on a support set;
  • gradient-based inner-loop adaptation.

If we do not separate them, almost any outcome can be post-rationalised as “meta-learning,” which is a wonderfully efficient way to learn nothing.

The idea already has ancestors

This question does not arrive on empty land. Schlag, Irie and Schmidhuber showed that linear transformers can be understood as fast-weight programmers. Von Oswald and colleagues constructed and trained transformers that implement gradient-descent-like learning in context. Ahn and colleagues showed that linear transformers can learn preconditioned gradient descent. More recent work has explicitly meta-tuned pretrained vision transformers across samples and tasks.

Those results make the mechanism plausible. They do not establish the causal bridge I care about here: that implicit inner learning is why the convnet–transformer ordering reverses as data increases.

There is a personal loop in this as well. In a research agenda I wrote in 2021, I proposed task-conditional query, key and value networks, along with hypernetwork-style positional embeddings. At the time I was asking how to condition a transformer more deeply on the task. The question now sits one level underneath: is data-conditioned inner learning already part of what makes the transformer win, and can we transfer that advantage to another architectural family?

I do not know if the answer is yes. But this gives us a better target than another argument about which backbone is “best.” Architectures do not have a single ordering independent of data, objective, adaptation budget and task structure.

The interesting object is the reversal.
