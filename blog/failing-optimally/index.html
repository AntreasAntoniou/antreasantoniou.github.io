Failing Optimally

The research craft of turning failed experiments into maximum information per unit of time.

Developed from a January 2025 long-form research conversation and expanded in the Research Archivum. Edited for clarity and factual precision.

Idea
Failure is not judgment; it is data about which part of the search space is empty.
Mechanism
Set kill criteria, seek early signal, and choose experiments that distinguish between hypotheses.
Implication
A good research culture does not avoid failure. It makes failure legible, cheap, and cumulative.

How to fail in ways that accelerate learning

Research is bungee jumping into the abyss.

You're at the edge of human knowledge, looking down into unknown territory. You jump. Sometimes the cord catches you. Sometimes you discover the cord was tied to nothing. Either way, you learn something about the abyss.

Most people find this terrifying. Researchers learn to find it exhilarating.

Failure as Information

Here's the mental model shift that makes research sustainable: failure isn't judgment. It's data.

When an experiment doesn't work, you haven't failed as a person. You've learned something about the world. That hypothesis was wrong. That approach doesn't work. That corner of the abyss is empty.

This sounds obvious when stated explicitly. But it's hard to internalize. Our educational systems train us to avoid failure. Wrong answers are punished. Mistakes are embarrassing. We learn to play it safe.

Research requires unlearning this. You have to be comfortable trying things that probably won't work, because the alternative—only trying safe things—means never discovering anything new.

The Exploration-Exploitation Trade-off

There's a classic problem in machine learning called exploration vs. exploitation. Do you keep doing what works (exploit), or try new things that might work better (explore)?

Research is heavily weighted toward exploration. You're not optimizing a known objective—you're discovering what objectives are even possible.

This means lots of failure. Most directions you explore lead nowhere. But the ones that lead somewhere are worth all the dead ends.

The skill is failing efficiently. Not avoiding failure—that's impossible. But failing in ways that maximize information gained per unit of time.

Failing Fast

If an approach isn't going to work, you want to know quickly. Spending six months on a doomed project is worse than spending two weeks on it.

This requires:

Clear success criteria: Define what would convince you the approach is working. And what would convince you it's not.

Early checkpoints: Don't wait until the end to evaluate. Set intermediate milestones that give signal earlier.

Intellectual honesty: When the signs are bad, don't rationalize. Don't add one more tweak hoping it'll fix everything. Recognize when to cut losses.

Bias toward action: Start experiments quickly. Real data beats speculation. You'll learn more from running a quick test than from months of theoretical analysis.

The Quarter-Checking Model

I think of failed experiments as checking quarters of the search space. Each failure eliminates possibilities.

Before the experiment: "The answer might be anywhere." After the experiment: "The answer definitely isn't there."

This is progress. It's not the progress we hoped for, but it's progress. The search space is smaller. The remaining possibilities are better.

Good researchers accumulate these negative results efficiently. They develop intuition for which quarters to check first—which failures will be most informative.

The Fear Problem

Many people avoid research because they're intimidated by the unknown. The abyss is scary when you haven't jumped before.

The cure is jumping. Not theory about jumping. Actual jumping.

Early jumps are terrifying. The cord catches you, or it doesn't, and either way you survive. After enough jumps, the fear becomes manageable. The abyss becomes familiar. Failure becomes routine.

This is why research apprenticeship matters. You need to fail a bunch of times in a low-stakes environment before you're ready to fail at the frontier.

The Team Dynamic

Optimal failure is easier with the right team.

You need people who:

  • Celebrate negative results (seriously celebrate—we learned something!)
  • Share failures openly rather than hiding them
  • Don't blame individuals for experiments that don't work
  • Ask "what did we learn?" rather than "whose fault was this?"

Teams that punish failure get less exploration. People play it safe. The work becomes incremental rather than transformative.

Teams that embrace failure get more swings at the plate. More misses, but also more home runs.

Applying This Outside Research

The failure mindset applies beyond formal research:

Startups: Most ideas don't work. Build a pipeline of experiments, not a single bet.

Career: Try things that might not work. A failed project that teaches you something is better than safe stagnation.

Learning: Struggle is signal. If you never fail at a problem, you're not pushing hard enough.

Relationships: Have uncomfortable conversations. Avoiding conflict isn't success—it's information avoidance.

The principle is general: when facing uncertainty, bias toward action and treat failure as data.

The abyss is less scary once you've jumped a few times.
